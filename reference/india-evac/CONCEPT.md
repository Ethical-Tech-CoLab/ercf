# Concept

## What this is

The **Evacuation Readiness & Uncertainty Simulator** is a browser-based research instrument built for a PhD project on civilian protection in armed conflict. It models the problem of assigning evacuee groups to destination sites under **incomplete, unreliable, or absent field intelligence** — the normal operating condition for humanitarian actors in active conflict, not an edge case.

It is a **conceptual demonstration tool**, not an operational planning system. All destinations, evacuee groups, and factor assessments are synthetic, generated by a seeded RNG. Nothing here is real location data, and no output should inform a real evacuation decision.

## The research questions

1. How does the multi-factor readiness of a potential evacuation destination translate into a probabilistic evacuation outcome?
2. How does degraded field intelligence — modelled as rising uncertainty — propagate through an evacuation decision chain?
3. Where would AI-assisted real-time assessment provide the greatest marginal gain in decision quality?

The tool's central argument: **the same assessed readiness scores produce materially different predicted success rates as uncertainty rises, even when no real-world condition has changed.** That gap — between what is true on the ground and what is knowable about it — is the thing AI-assisted field assessment is positioned to close. The simulator makes that gap visible and measurable rather than asserting it.

## The readiness model

Each destination is scored across seven factors. Three are **gatekeepers** (double-weighted, and if blocked, hard-cap readiness at 20% regardless of every other score):

- **Security** — a site under active threat cannot be made viable by good food supply.
- **Authority consent** — without host-authority agreement, movement is legally precarious.
- **Willingness** — a host community that has explicitly refused is as disqualifying as a blocked checkpoint. This factor was promoted from standard to gatekeeper status specifically because modelling it as weight-1 let strong shelter/food/medical scores mathematically outweigh an outright refusal — understating an exclusion that, in practice, is total.

The remaining four (Capacity, Shelter, Food & water, Medical capacity) are standard-weighted and degrade readiness proportionally rather than capping it.

## Two epistemic distinctions the model insists on

**Known unknown vs. unknown unknown.** A factor assessed "Unknown" with high confidence (we're sure we don't know) scores higher than the same status with low confidence (we're not even sure our ignorance is accurate). A field team that explicitly flags a gap is providing more actionable information than one operating under conditions where its own reporting can't be trusted.

**Unknown vs. Unwilling.** These are not the same failure mode. "Unknown" (willingness not yet assessed) is an intelligence gap — closeable by better field assessment. "Unwilling" (a confirmed `blocked` status on the Willingness factor, displayed with that specific label rather than the generic "Blocked") is a confirmed exclusion — no amount of additional intelligence changes the outcome. Conflating the two would make an unresolvable refusal look like a solvable information problem, which is precisely the kind of error the tool exists to surface rather than commit.

## Uncertainty, twice over

Uncertainty operates at two levels that multiply together: a **base confidence** per factor (how well-assessed was this specific thing), and a global **Field Uncertainty** slider (how degraded is the whole information environment right now). Monte Carlo simulation (500 runs per group–destination pair) turns that combined uncertainty into an outcome distribution — a success rate and a standard deviation — rather than a single point estimate that hides how fragile the assignment actually is.

## Intended use vs. scope

This is illustrative, not predictive. It is designed to make an uncertainty-propagation argument legible for thesis examiners and stakeholders, and to identify — via the Factor Information Value panel — which unknowns, if resolved, would move outcomes the most. It is not calibrated against empirical field data, and every numeric threshold (40% viability floor, 0.85 max perturbation probability, the specific factor weights) is a modelling assumption open to challenge, documented as such in `Evacuation_Simulator_Methodology.docx`.
