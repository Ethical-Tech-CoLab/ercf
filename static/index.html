Scenario Parameters
(elderly · disabled · children · medical)
Vulnerable population includes: elderly (65+), people with disabilities, children under 5, pregnant and lactating women, and individuals with chronic medical conditions. Typically 20–40% of conflict-affected populations.
Children under 5 specifically require:
  • 1 adult escort per child (reduces effective transport capacity)
  • Medical monitoring every 4–6 hours (increases paramedic requirements)
  • Specialized nutrition (RUTF, therapeutic milk) not in standard ration
  • Psychological support on arrival
Sources: Sphere 2018 Vulnerable Populations chapter; UNICEF Child Protection in Emergencies 2022.
7-Dimension Risk Indicators (1=Minimal · 5=Critical)
D1 Kinetic ThreatAirstrikes · artillery · proximity
3.0
1
Minimal — No active armed conflict in the area. Last incident over 6 months ago.
2
Low — Sporadic incidents reported, conflict front line more than 100 km (62 mi) away. No impact on planned route.
3
Moderate — Intermittent combat or airstrikes within 50 km (31 mi). Operations possible but require monitoring.
4
High — Active airstrikes or artillery in the operational area. Movement requires armed escort or significant route change.
5
Critical — Direct fire on civilians in the area. No safe window for movement. Immediate suspension of operations required. At D1 ≥ 4, the evacuation window indicator switches to NO SAFE EVACUATION WINDOW — no countdown is shown because movement itself is the hazard.
D2 Mobility ConstraintsWounded · bedridden · no transport · movement barriers
3.0
1
Minimal — Population is fully mobile. No physical movement barriers identified. Medical vehicle multiplier ×0.8.
2
Low — Some individuals require assistance but majority can self-evacuate with minimal support. Medical vehicle multiplier ×1.0 (baseline).
3
Moderate — Significant portion requires physical assistance or adapted transport. Movement is slow. Medical vehicle multiplier ×1.3.
4
High — Large number of bedridden, critically wounded, or immobile individuals. Standard transport insufficient. Medical vehicle multiplier ×1.8.
5
Critical — Mass casualty or complete mobility collapse. Self-evacuation impossible. Full medical extraction required. Medical vehicle multiplier ×2.5.
D3 AuthorizationParty consent · ceasefire · IHL compliance
3.0
1
Minimal — Full consent from all parties. Formal agreement in place. No legal or political obstacles.
2
Low — Verbal agreement obtained from key parties. Written confirmation pending but not expected to fail.
3
Moderate — Authorization from one party only. Other parties have not confirmed but have not actively opposed.
4
High — Consent contested or revoked by at least one armed actor. Significant IHL compliance concerns.
5
Critical — Active refusal or obstruction by a party. No valid authorization. Operation would violate IHL or expose civilians to retaliation.
D4 LogisticsRoads · transport · fuel · checkpoints
3.0
1
Minimal — All routes passable. Sufficient vehicles and fuel. No checkpoints or administrative obstacles.
2
Low — Minor road damage or 1–2 checkpoints expected. Route viable with standard planning.
3
Moderate — Partial road destruction, fuel scarcity, or multiple hostile checkpoints. Requires contingency route and pre-negotiated passage.
4
High — Primary route blocked. Only high-risk alternatives available. Fuel access critical. Convoy likely to be stopped or delayed significantly.
5
Critical — No viable ground route. Complete logistical breakdown. Requires airlift, waterway, or operation must be postponed.
D5 DestinationShelter · food · water · medical capacity
3.0
1
Minimal — Destination fully equipped. Shelter, food, water, and medical care available for full population load.
2
Low — Destination functional but operating near capacity. Minor gaps manageable with pre-positioning.
3
Moderate — Destination partially functional. Critical gaps in shelter or medical care. Requires parallel humanitarian prepositioning.
4
High — Destination overwhelmed or insecure. Arriving population at immediate risk of exposure, malnutrition, or violence.
5
Critical — Destination is unsafe or non-existent. Evacuating population would be placed in equal or greater danger than origin.
D6 UrgencyImminent attack · window closing · timeline
3.0
1
Minimal — No immediate pressure. Operation can be planned and executed over days or weeks without risk escalation.
2
Low — Situation is stable but deteriorating. Window of 5–7 days before conditions worsen significantly.
3
Moderate — Operational window is 48–72 hours. Ceasefire holding but fragile. Action required within days.
4
High — Window closing within 24 hours. Imminent offensive, flooding, or access denial expected. Rapid deployment required.
5
Critical — Imminent threat to life. Hours or less to act. Any delay results in mass casualties or complete loss of access.
D7 InformationComms reliability · disinfo · civilian awareness
3.0
1
Minimal — Reliable communications with all actors and affected population. No significant disinformation environment.
2
Low — Communications intermittent but functional. Population generally informed. Minor rumors manageable.
3
Moderate — Significant communications gaps. Active disinformation campaigns. Civilian awareness of evacuation low or distorted.
4
High — Communications largely down. Population misinformed or unaware. Hostile narratives actively undermining civilian trust in operation.
5
Critical — Complete communications blackout. No channel to reach population or coordinate with parties. Operation is effectively blind.

⚠️ Model Limitation: Conflict Pattern not reflected in resource costs
The Conflict Pattern parameter (Urban Siege, Enclave/Blockade, City Conflict, Regional/Dispersed) currently affects only the mortality and stay-or-evacuate model — it does not adjust evacuation resource costs (security personnel ratios, armoured vehicle requirements, or route length multipliers). In high-intensity conflict contexts (Urban Siege, Enclave/Blockade), actual security and transport costs may be substantially higher than shown. No primary source with validated numerical multipliers by conflict type was identified in the humanitarian operations literature; implementing unvalidated multipliers was judged to risk misleading precision. This limitation should be considered when using cost estimates for Urban Siege or Enclave/Blockade scenarios.

What this model is for
  • Prospective operational planning: estimating the human cost of inaction before a crisis escalates — not retrospective mortality forecasting.
  • Planning horizon: 0–90 days. Accuracy degrades for very long durations (>120 days) due to linear accumulation.
  • Decision support: when and whether to evacuate. Financial cost estimates are substantially more reliable than death and injury projections.

Known limitations
  • Sudan and Gaza exceed model scope: Sudan is a conflict dispersed across 1.8M km² (continental scale); Gaza mortality significantly driven by famine/healthcare collapse. Both require purpose-built models.
  • Srebrenica (genocide): targeted mass killing in 3 days. Model assumes conflict attrition over time. Genocide dynamics are categorically different and not modeled.
  • Famine / disease mortality not included: blockade-induced starvation and healthcare collapse are absent. This is the primary source of underestimation in prolonged enclave scenarios.
  • Sample size n=10. No confidence intervals. All parameters are modelled estimates, not statistically derived.
  • Siege cap (pop ≤ 500k): heuristic threshold. Gaza (2.3M) is excluded from siege cap even though movement was under fire, resulting in some underprotection correction.
How to use this responsibly
Treat death and injury estimates as order-of-magnitude indicators, not precise forecasts. A ratio of 2× to 5× vs the historical record is typical for the best-calibrated cases. Financial cost estimates are substantially more reliable. Always validate against country-specific intelligence and operational context before any operational application.

Mortality Model Methodology
Method
ERCF Mortality Model v4. Empirically-derived base rates from 10 historical conflict cases (1995–2024). Dimensional modulation via D3×D4 confinement score (×0.5–×8) and D1-population auto-exposure factor (log¹·⁴ falloff). Siege protection cap applied to urban conflicts ≤500k population (D3≤2 AND D1≥4: max_protection=0.30). Auto mode recommended for prospective planning; named Conflict Pattern for retrospective calibration. Calibration: 2/10 cases within 2× in auto mode (Kherson 0.59×, Mosul 0.71×); 3 unique cases within 2× across both modes (add DRC-Goma named 1.68×). Full calibration data in the Historical Cases tab → case detail panel.

📋 ERCF — Methodology & Model Documentation
Scope: The ERCF is a decision-support tool for prospective operational planning over a 0–90 day window in active conflict zones. It is not a forecasting model, a real-time monitoring tool, or a substitute for field assessment. All outputs are planning-order-of-magnitude estimates unless explicitly marked as validated.
1 · Seven-Dimension Risk Framework

Risk is assessed across seven dimensions (D1–D7). Each is scored 1 (minimal) to 5 (critical). All weights are modelled estimates — no published IHL framework specifies numerical weights for civilian evacuation decisions.

3 · Mortality Model (v4)

The mortality model estimates cumulative civilian deaths and injuries over a given duration. It uses a base mortality rate per risk level, modulated by conflict pattern (exposure factor), population size, and time.

  • Base rates by level: L0: 0.0002%/day · L1: 0.0005%/day · L2: 0.001%/day · L3: 0.003%/day · L4: 0.008%/day (estimated, calibrated against 10 historical cases)
  • Injury ratio: 4:1 injuries per death (ICRC field data, standard planning assumption)
  • Calibration: Tested against 10 conflict cases (1995–2024). 2/10 cases fall within 2× of recorded deaths in Auto mode. 3/10 across both Auto and Urban Siege modes. Srebrenica is excluded from calibration as a mass atrocity/genocide — the mortality process is categorically different from conflict attrition.
  • Known limitation: The model captures kinetic mortality rates but not infrastructure-denial mortality (starvation, waterborne disease, hypothermia). This produces systematic underestimation in siege contexts (e.g. Mariupol, Aleppo).

5 · Air & Walking Evacuation Modules

Fixed-wing aircraft: $2.08/passenger-km (2023) or $1.98/pax-km (2024). Source: WFP Executive Board WFP/EB.A/2025/8-D (10 Jun 2025), UNHAS fleet-wide operational cost. Validated.

Helicopter (Mi-8/17 class): $2.22/pax-km central estimate (range $1.73–$3.11). Derived from UN Procurement Division contract PD/C0164/14 (Mi-8MTV, MINUSCA, USD 5.59M/year) ÷ estimated 600–1,080 flight hours/year. 50% load factor applied (conservative for emergency ops). Estimated.

Walking evacuation: Base speed 8 km/h (Flee migration model, arXiv:1903.08255), adjusted downward by vulnerable population % using UK Health Survey for England gait-speed data for adults 65+ (~3 km/h floor). 8 effective hours/day assumed (conservative — excludes night movement in conflict zones). Daily cost: $3.50/person/day UNHCR baseline + $92/day MSF rate for personnel. Estimated.

6 · Cost of Civilians Remaining (Assistance Model)
  • Base supply cost: $3.50/person/day (UNHCR Baseline Operational Cost, existing ERCF anchor)
  • Access overhead: scaled by D4 (logistics penalty +10–60%), D3 (access +10%), D7 (coordination +5%), and terrain multiplier
  • Emergency extraction probability: modelled as 1 − exp(−k × days), calibrated against Mariupol, Aleppo, Mosul, DRC, and CAR cases. D1 and D3 modulate the probability.
  • Field medical cost: $1,200/injury base (WHO Emergency Health cluster), scaled by D5 destination medical capacity (×1.0 at D5=5 to ×2.0 at D5=1).
  • Financial estimate (separate): Risk-scaled survival estimate $1–$12/person/day by level — simpler model used for the time-series chart only, not for the full assistance cost.

8 · Terrain & Seasonal Constraints

Terrain multipliers (×1.0 to ×4.0) are applied to transport and fuel costs. Source basis: World Bank HDM-4 vehicle operating cost relationships (roughness-to-cost) and NRC/Protect Humanitarian Space (2024) qualitative H2R cost ranges. No primary source provides validated numerical multipliers by terrain category for humanitarian evacuation specifically.

Seasonal closure periods are estimated from latitude zone: northern temperate (lat>30°): Dec–Mar; southern temperate (lat<-30°): Jun–Sep; tropical (-30° to 30°): Apr–Oct (broad approximation). Closure boosts: terrain L1 +50%, L2 +30%, L3 +20%. These are planning-order-of-magnitude estimates.

9 · Historical Calibration Cases

The model is calibrated against 10 conflict cases (1995–2024): Mariupol (2022), Gaza (2023–), Srebrenica (1995), Aleppo (2012–16), Kherson (2022), Mosul (2016–17), Kosovo (1999), CAR (2014), Sudan/Khartoum (2023–), and DRC/Goma (2024). Calibration accuracy: 2/10 within 2× of documented deaths in Auto mode. Srebrenica is included as a documented boundary case (mass atrocity, not modellable by attrition).

Geographic Calibration Limitation: The calibration dataset is predominantly drawn from Middle East and Eastern Europe conflicts (where primary source documentation meeting ERCF criteria is most available). Sub-Saharan Africa is represented by 2 cases (CAR, DRC/Goma, Angola/Huambo), Latin America by 0 cases, and South/Central Asia by 0 cases outside Iraq/Afghanistan. This reflects a real documentation gap — verified civilian death counts with bounded populations and durations ≤90 days are rare in these regions — not a research omission. When applying ERCF to Sub-Saharan Africa or Latin America contexts, treat cost and mortality estimates with additional caution and seek local field data to supplement model outputs.
